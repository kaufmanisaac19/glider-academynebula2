The big idea
Speed, distance, and time are three sides of the same idea. Know any two — you always find the third.
Every trip a glider makes is described by three numbers: how far it went (distance), how long it took (time), and how fast it was going (speed). Change one and the others must respond. This relationship runs inside ArduPlane every time it plans a route.
The core relationship: Distance = Speed × Time. It works all three ways — rearrange it to find whichever value you're missing.
Why this matters for your build: When ArduPlane plans a waypoint route, it calculates required speed from distance and time budget. This formula is literally what runs inside your future flight controller.

Distance
Distance is simply how much ground got covered — from start to finish.
If your glider flies at a steady 10 m/s for 5 seconds, it covers 50 metres. Fly for 10 seconds at the same speed and it covers 100 metres — twice as much. More time at the same speed always means more distance. Drag the slider to see the trail grow.
The pattern: Double the time → double the distance. Half the time → half the distance. Distance and time are directly linked when speed is constant.

Speed
Speed is how many metres you cover every single second.
A glider at 10 m/s covers 10 metres in second 1, another 10 in second 2, another 10 in second 3. Speed is a rate — how much distance per second. Two gliders fly for the same time below — watch how far apart they end up just from different speeds.
Speed is the multiplier that turns time into distance. Higher speed = each second of flight covers more ground. Same time, wildly different distances.

The big idea
Algebra is rearranging. One formula — used three different ways.
Every engineering calculation you'll ever do is just taking a formula and rearranging it to find the missing piece. You already know Distance = Speed × Time. From that one formula, you can find Speed and Time too — just by covering the variable you want and reading what remains. This is the only algebra skill you need right now.
The triangle method: Write D on top, S and T side by side on the bottom (like a fraction with a multiplication sign). Cover whichever variable you want to find. What remains tells you exactly what to do: D covered = S×T, S covered = D÷T, T covered = D÷S.
This is real engineering: Every formula your flight controller runs is rearranged versions of basic relationships. Once you can rearrange, you can derive any variation you need on the fly.

Hover thrust
The first real engineering calculation for your build: how much thrust do you need to hover?
Thrust needed to hover = mass × 9.8. This comes from Force = mass × acceleration, where the acceleration is gravity (9.8 m/s²). For hover, the upward force (thrust) must exactly equal the downward force (weight). Drag the mass slider and watch the required thrust update in real time.
Why this matters now: When you go to select a motor for your custom glider, the first thing you check is whether it produces enough thrust to lift the aircraft. If the aircraft weighs 0.5 kg, you need at least 4.9 N of thrust just to hover — and typically 2× that for adequate climb performance.

Rule of thumb: For good climb performance, your motors should produce 2× the hover thrust at full power. So a 0.5 kg glider wants motors capable of ~10 N total thrust max. This is why motor selection starts with weighing your aircraft.
Range formula
Range = altitude × glide ratio. Rearrange it and you can plan any motor-off glide mission.
Three versions of the same formula: Range = altitude × glide ratio | Altitude = range ÷ glide ratio | Glide ratio = range ÷ altitude. All three are the same equation rearranged. Pick the one where you know two values and want the third.
Mission example: You want to silently overfly a 2.5 km stretch with motor off. Your glider has a 1:12 glide ratio. What altitude do you need to start the glide? Altitude = range ÷ glide ratio = 2500 ÷ 12 = 208 m. Launch from 210 m to have a small safety margin.

The most important number
Glide ratio is how far your aircraft travels forward for every metre it descends. It defines your mission capability.
A glide ratio of 1:12 means 12 metres forward per 1 metre descended. A sailplane might achieve 1:50. The Ranger 600 you'll start with achieves about 1:10. Your future custom design should target 1:15 or better. The difference between 1:10 and 1:15 is not small — from 200 m altitude it's the difference between 2 km and 3 km of silent range.
The formula: Glide ratio = forward distance ÷ altitude lost. Or: forward distance = altitude × ratio number. Or: altitude needed = target distance ÷ ratio number. All three rearrangements of the same relationship.
Tactical value: Your glider can cut the motor at altitude, silently overfly a target area, and relight the motor for return. The higher your glide ratio, the more area you can cover silently. This is a direct engineering number that determines mission utility.

What determines glide ratio
Three things: wing shape, aspect ratio, and airframe cleanliness. All three reduce drag.
Glide ratio is essentially a measure of how efficiently your aircraft converts altitude into forward motion. The only thing wasting that energy is drag. So improving glide ratio means reducing drag in all its forms: induced drag (from the wings generating lift) and parasitic drag (from everything that isn't the wings).
Wing shape: A well-curved airfoil section generates lift more efficiently than a flat or poorly designed one, reducing the induced drag for a given lift force.
Aspect ratio: Longer thinner wings have less induced drag. Every time you double the aspect ratio, you roughly halve the induced drag. This is why sailplanes look like flying rulers.
Airframe cleanliness: Every bump, wire, joint, and protrusion adds parasitic drag. A clean tight fuselage, flush motor mount, and no hanging wires can add 15–20% to your glide ratio over a sloppy build.

Mission planning
Glide ratio directly determines what missions your aircraft can fly.
Different missions need different glide ratios. A short reconnaissance pass might need 1:8. A long silent overfly mission needs 1:15+. Understanding the required glide ratio for your target mission tells you exactly what wing design you need — before you cut a single piece of foam.
Working backward: Define the mission first. "I need to silently overfly a 3 km stretch starting from 250 m altitude." Required glide ratio = 3000 ÷ 250 = 12. So you need 1:12 minimum, target 1:15 for safety margin.
Safety margin: Always design for better glide ratio than the minimum required. Thermal conditions, wind, and small design imperfections will all reduce your actual glide ratio below the theoretical value.

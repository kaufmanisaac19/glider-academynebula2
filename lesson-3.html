The big idea
Acceleration is any change in speed — speeding up, slowing down, or changing direction.
In Lesson 1 you learned speed. In Lesson 2 you learned forces. Now they connect: when a net force acts on your glider it does not instantly change speed — it accelerates. That word just means speed is changing. Speeding up is acceleration. Slowing down is acceleration too.
Newton in plain English: A net force always causes acceleration. Zero net force means zero acceleration — the glider holds constant speed. Your flight controller fights to keep net force at zero during cruise.
Why this matters: Your ArduPlane IMU reads acceleration 400 times per second. It integrates that to get velocity, then integrates again to get position. This chain runs constantly during every autonomous flight.

Speed builds gradually when you apply throttle — that gradual build IS acceleration. When cut, drag decelerates it gradually. The rate of change is the acceleration.
Net force causes acceleration
A net force always causes acceleration. Zero net force — zero acceleration — constant speed.
When thrust exceeds drag, the leftover forward force — the net force — causes the glider to accelerate. When thrust exactly equals drag, net force is zero and the glider holds constant speed. This is cruise. Drag the slider to find the balance point.
The cruise insight: At cruise, net force = 0. The glider does not need force to keep moving — only to change speed. The motor is just replacing energy lost to drag, nothing more.

Speeding up
Acceleration is highest when speed is lowest — and fades as you approach cruise speed.
At the start of the takeoff roll, speed is near zero so drag is nearly zero. Almost all thrust becomes net force — maximum acceleration. As speed builds, drag catches up, net force shrinks, acceleration fades — even though you are still getting faster.
The counterintuitive part: The glider accelerates fastest at the moment it looks most stationary. Once it is moving quickly it is barely accelerating at all. Full throttle the whole time — drag just catches up.

The glide
Cut the motor. Your glider does not fall — it glides. A quadcopter falls straight down.
When the motor is cut there is no thrust. But the wing still generates lift from forward airflow. The glider descends at a shallow angle — trading altitude for forward distance. The better the wing design, the shallower that angle and the further it travels.
Glide ratio in practice: A 1:12 ratio means 1,200 m of range from 100 m altitude with no motor at all. Silent, efficient, and if the motor fails — you have a controlled landing instead of a crash.
Mission value: Cut the motor at altitude, silently overfly a target area, then relight the motor for the return trip. This is a real tactical capability that comes directly from good glide ratio design.

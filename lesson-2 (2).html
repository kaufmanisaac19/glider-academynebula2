The big idea
A force is any push or pull. That's all it ever is.
When you push a door open — force. When a magnet pulls a paperclip — force. When the earth pulls everything downward — force, and we call it gravity. Forces are what cause things to start moving, stop moving, speed up, or slow down. Without a force, an object just keeps doing whatever it was already doing forever.
Newton's first law in plain English: An object stays still until a force acts on it. A moving object keeps moving at the same speed in the same direction until a force acts on it. Forces are the only things that change motion.
Why this matters: Every single thing your flight controller does is about managing forces. It adjusts motor speed and control surfaces to control the four forces acting on your glider every second of every flight.

Gravity
Gravity is the earth pulling everything toward its center — constantly, downward, always.
The heavier an object, the harder gravity pulls it. We measure force in Newtons (N). On Earth, gravity pulls with about 9.8 N for every kilogram of mass. A 2 kg glider gets pulled down with 19.6 N — twice as hard as a 1 kg glider. Drag the slider to see the gravity arrow grow with mass.
Newtons explained: 1 Newton is roughly the force of a small apple resting on your hand. Your glider weighing 0.5 kg has gravity pulling it down with about 4.9 N — 4.9 apples' worth of force, constantly downward.

The weight spiral: Every gram you add to your glider is weight your motor has to lift. Heavier → needs more thrust → needs bigger motor → needs bigger battery → which adds even more weight. Keeping your build light is the most important design principle you'll learn.
Thrust
Thrust is the force your propeller creates by pushing air backward — which pushes the aircraft forward and upward.
When a propeller spins, it pushes air backward. By Newton's third law — every action has an equal and opposite reaction — the aircraft gets pushed forward. The faster the propeller spins, the more air it moves per second, and the more thrust it creates. Drag the throttle slider and watch thrust fight gravity.
The hover point: When thrust exactly equals the downward pull of gravity, the aircraft neither rises nor falls. This is hover. Your future autopilot adjusts motor speed hundreds of times per second hunting for this balance point.

Four forces
Every flying object deals with exactly four forces — all at once, all the time.
You've met gravity and thrust. The other two: Drag is air resistance — it pushes back against any object moving through air. Lift is the upward force generated by the wing shape as it moves through air. Toggle each force off below and watch what happens to the aircraft.
Fixed-wing advantage: A quadcopter must generate all of its upward force with thrust alone — very inefficient. Your glider uses its wing to generate lift, carrying most of the aircraft weight. The motor only needs to overcome drag. This is why gliders can fly for so much longer on the same battery.
Toggle forces on and off
All four active: Normal flight. Thrust pushes forward, gravity pulls down, drag resists forward motion, lift counteracts gravity. When balanced, the glider cruises steadily.
